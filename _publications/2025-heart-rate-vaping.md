<h3>üìù Abstract</h3>
<p class="abstract-text">
<strong>Background:</strong> Electronic cigarettes and nicotine vaping have become increasingly prevalent, yet the physiological responses associated with vaping events remain understudied. Wearable devices offer a promising avenue for passively monitoring these responses in real-world settings.<br><br>
<strong>Objectives:</strong> This study aims to identify and characterize features of heart rate data that are statistically associated with nicotine vaping events, leveraging data collected from wearable sensors during ecological momentary assessment (EMA) protocols.<br><br>
<strong>Methods:</strong> We applied a suite of applied statistical methods including Kalman filtering for signal smoothing, anomaly detection algorithms, and mixed-effects models to analyze continuous heart rate time series data. Features extracted included heart rate variability metrics, mean elevation, and temporal patterns surrounding self-reported vaping events.<br><br>
<strong>Results:</strong> Several heart rate features showed statistically significant associations with nicotine vaping events, including acute elevations in mean heart rate and changes in short-term heart rate variability. These physiological signatures were detectable in the minutes surrounding self-reported vaping events.<br><br>
<strong>Conclusions:</strong> Statistical analysis of wearable heart rate data can yield meaningful physiological biomarkers of nicotine vaping. These findings support the feasibility of passive, wearable-based monitoring as a tool in tobacco control research.
</p>
